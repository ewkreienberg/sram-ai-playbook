**Data engineering is the bigger bottleneck.** The AI models themselves are increasingly accessible — the tools exist, the APIs exist, the foundation models exist. Hammerhead has some of it. The core SRAM IT organization has some of it. Manufacturing has some of it. Nobody owns it end to end across the enterprise. That's the single biggest internal capability gap for AI adoption and frankly it's upstream of AI — it's a data infrastructure problem that exists regardless of whether AI is in the picture.

**AI product management is essentially absent.** Knowing how to scope an AI pilot responsibly — defining the right guardrails, designing the human review workflow, choosing the right evaluation metrics, managing the rollout without destroying internal trust if the first output is wrong — that's a specific discipline. **Change management capability is limited.** The failed initiatives I described earlier didn't fail primarily because of technical problems. They failed because we didn't have people skilled at driving adoption across a product-first culture that is inherently skeptical of process change. AI deployments that touch customer-facing workflows will face exactly that resistance again.

**The right resourcing model given those gaps:**

My honest view is a hybrid. I'd argue against two extremes. The first extreme is building everything in-house from scratch. Our competitive advantage is the product ecosystem, the brand relationships, the channel depth, and the connected rider data. AI is a capability that serves those advantages — it's not the advantage itself. Fully building from scratch is too slow, too expensive, and pulls talent away from where our real differentiation lives.

The second extreme is buying a turnkey AI solution and dropping it on top of our existing systems. You get a demo that looks impressive and an integration that takes eighteen months and costs three times what was quoted and produces outputs that your support team doesn't trust because they weren't involved in building it.

The right model is a small internal AI product team — maybe four to six people — who own the use case definition, the data readiness assessment, the vendor evaluation, and the change management. These people speak both business and technology. They don't build the models themselves — they select and integrate best-in-class external tools and foundation models where appropriate. They own the deployment and the human review workflow. Surrounding that internal team you use implementation partners for the heavy data engineering lift and foundation model providers for the actual AI capability layer.

**The executive sponsorship question is real.** For this to work at the scale we're describing, somebody at the C-suite level needs to own it. Not as a side responsibility — as a primary accountability. Whether that's a Chief Digital Officer, a Chief Data Officer, or Ken himself deciding to make it his personal priority — that decision needs to be made explicitly. Because if it's nobody's primary job, it becomes everybody's secondary job, and we already know what happens then.
