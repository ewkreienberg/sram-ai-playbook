## Interview: Plan A, Plan B, and Plan C
Our team picked SRAM LLC for the AI Adoption Playbook. They are a privately held bicycle components company out of Chicago with nine brands across drivetrain, suspension, wheels, power meters, and cycling computers. Early on, our teammate Will Zheng got us connected with Clint Weber, SRAM's VP of Global Sales and Manufacturing, and he agreed to do a proper sit-down interview with us about how SRAM thinks about management, innovation, and where AI fits in. 

However, a few days before the interview, Clint let us know he had to fly to Taiwan on short notice which left us without an interviewee about a week and a half before the assignment was due. Plan A fell through.

We started reaching out to other leaders at SRAM. We direct messaged leaders on LinkedIn, sent emails, and worked whatever connections we had. Some were names Clint himself had suggested as people worth talking to about AI and operational challenges. The rest were cold messages to SRAM people in sales, manufacturing, engineering, and corporate strategy. Altogether we reached out to about 40 distinct people. Yeah, thats right, 40, and no responses at all. Plan B fell through.

 We also found out a team in the other section of this course had been reaching out to SRAM contacts too, and they got the same result, nothing. Between both teams, we kept trying on Plan B by reaching out to connections at competitors like Shimano, but no luck there either. Eventually, we had to move on and figure out a Plan C.

Will Zheng is a member of our project team, and he also happens to be a close friend of Clint Weber. They have been riding bikes together for years and over the course of all those rides, Will and Clint have talked a lot about what is going on at SRAM, what the biggest operational headaches are, where AI might fit, and how leadership thinks about prioritization and change.

Interviewing a teammate is obviously not the same as interviewing someone at the company. We know that, but Will had picked up a lot of real context from Clint over the years, and given our situation, his perspective was the best window into SRAM's thinking we were going to get. We sat down with Will to see how SRAM was approaching AI strategically, what stands in the way, and how you get a company full of engineers to care about process improvement. It was not a perfect substitute for talking to Clint directly, but it gave us something real to work from.

## What We Learned

We went into the interview with three main threads we wanted to pull on -- how SRAM thinks about AI at a strategic level, what the practical blockers are in terms of culture and tech stack, and how adoption would work inside a company whose culture is built around product engineering. 

**How SRAM thinks about AI strategy.** We started by asking Will how SRAM's corporate strategy side is approaching AI. He told us the board is pushing hard for an AI strategy and they want to understand two things -- how AI improves the financial picture, and how it keeps SRAM competitive long-term. But Will was quick to say that having a framework is not the same as being ready to execute. The real question SRAM is working through is about dependencies. They do not have clean, structured customer data right now. They do not have a deep AI talent bench. The tech stack is not set up for advanced analytics. So the thinking, as Will described it, is about staging -- figure out what you do today that does not depend on any of those things, get some early wins on the board, and then spend the next two to three years building the foundation so the harder stuff becomes feasible. We identified Customer Support as the one angle that does not need a deep bench or broad data for AI to make meaningul progress, unlike other angles of supply chain, engineering, and product innovation.

**Top-down versus bottom-up.** We asked Will how AI tools would roll out at SRAM -- whether it would come from the top or bubble up within teams. He said it has to be both, and for different reasons. Productivity tools like Copilot and ChatGPT are top-down. You make them available company-wide, run training, and let people get comfortable using AI for everyday work like email, document review, and meeting notes. The goal there is to build baseline AI literacy and free up enough time for people to start noticing where else AI might help. But figuring out the deeper, more specific use cases is a different story. Leadership has a view of where the biggest revenue and cost drivers are, but employees on the ground see problems leadership does not. Will was emphatic about this -- there are blind spots at the top, and some of the best ideas for AI use cases will come from the people closest to the work. Both streams need to feed into the same prioritization conversation.

**Change management and finding early adopters.** Will pushed back on framing AI adoption as a technology problem and said it is fundamentally a change management problem. He described a process he called "scan, try, buy" -- you scan for promising ideas, you try them out with early adopters in different business units, and you scale the ones that work. The early adopters, he said, tend to be people in the middle of the organization -- not the department heads and not the most junior people, but the ones who understand both the technology and the business process. Those are the people who become your internal champions and help carry adoption forward organically when it is time to go broader. Will talked about them as "mavens" who spread the word through credibility rather than authority.

**Why quick wins are not optional.** One of our teammates asked how important it is to show a quick win before going after the bigger AI initiatives. Will did not hedge -- he said quick wins are essential. They give you checkpoints on a multi-year strategy so the organization knows whether things are on track. They build AI literacy across the company by showing people in concrete terms what AI does well and where it falls short. And they surface who the early adopters are, so when it is time to deploy something more ambitious in year two or three, you already know who to start with.

**Where SRAM wants to end up.** Will described SRAM's long-term AI ambition as augmenting innovation. The productivity and cost stuff is where you start, but the real goal is helping SRAM build more intellectual property and bring new products to market faster. He talked about SRAM eventually building an intelligent platform around its connected ecosystem -- where the product is not only hardware but a data-driven experience for the rider. Getting there depends on the machine learning and predictive analytics capabilities that require clean data, the right people, and a mature tech stack, which is exactly what the early-phase work is meant to build toward. Will laid it out in three buckets -- productivity and early wins first, then cost reductions and revenue improvements, and then continuous innovation as the long-term differentiator.

## From Will's Interview to a Simulated Diagnosis (attached simulation)

Will's interview gave us a strong sense of how SRAM's leadership thinks about AI, but we still did not have the level of operational detail that a direct conversation with Clint would have provided. To fill in the gaps, we took what Will told us and combined it with the competitive intelligence research we had been doing independently to build a simulated interview with Clint Weber. The simulated interview walks through the questions we would have asked Clint, with responses grounded in public research and the context Will shared from his conversations with Clint over the years.

We want to be upfront -- the simulated interview is not real. It carries a clear disclaimer saying it is AI-generated and does not represent Clint's actual views. We used it as a research tool to pressure-test our assumptions about SRAM's pain points, AI readiness, and how implementation should be sequenced before writing the final playbook.

We would have preferred a real interview with someone inside SRAM. We reached out to about 40 people trying to make it happen and got nowhere. So we worked with what we had. It is not perfect, but we think the combination of Will's real-world perspective and our research gave us a grounded enough picture to write a playbook worth reading.
